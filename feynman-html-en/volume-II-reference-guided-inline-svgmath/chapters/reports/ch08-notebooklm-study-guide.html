Chapter 8: Electrostatic Energy Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of electrostatic energy, ranging from the fundamental interactions between point charges to the energy stored in electric fields and its applications in atomic and nuclear physics.

Key Concepts

1. The Energy of Charge Systems

The electrostatic energy of a system of particles is defined as the work required to assemble the charges from an infinite distance.

  • Pairwise Interaction: For two charges q1q_1 and q2q_2 separated by distance r12r_{12}, the energy is U=q1q24πϵ0r12U = \frac{q_1q_2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r_{12}}.
  • Multiple Charges: The total energy is the sum of the mutual interaction of every possible pair of charges in the system.
  • Uniform Sphere of Charge: The energy required to assemble a sphere of radius aa with total charge QQ and uniform density ρ\rho is U=35Q24πϵ0aU = \frac{3}{5} \frac{Q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 a}.

2. Energy in Condensers and Conductors

Energy is stored when work is performed to transfer charge between conductors.

  • Condenser Energy: The energy of a charged condenser with capacity CC, charge QQ, and potential VV is expressed as U=12Q2CU = \frac{1}{2} \frac{Q^2}{C} or U=12CV2U = \frac{1}{2} CV^2.
  • Virtual Work: Mechanical forces between conductors can be determined by calculating the change in electrostatic energy (ΔU\Delta U) resulting from a small displacement (Δz\Delta z). For a parallel-plate condenser, the force is F=Q22ϵ0AF = \frac{Q^2}{2\epsilon_0 A}.
  • Average Field: The force on a conductor's surface charge is F=12QE0F = \frac{1}{2}QE_0. The factor of 1/21/2 arises because the field varies from zero inside the conductor to E0E_0 outside; the charges effectively experience the average field, E0/2E_0/2.

3. Ionic Crystals and Atomic Physics

Electrostatic energy is a primary component of the binding energy in ionic solids.

  • NaCl Structure: Sodium chloride forms a cubic lattice of alternating positive and negative ions.
  • Madelung Summation: The total energy of the crystal is found by summing the potential energies of all ion pairs. For NaCl, the electrostatic energy per molecule is approximately U=−1.747e2aU = -1.747 \frac{e^2}{a}.
  • Discrepancies: Theoretical calculations often differ slightly from experimental values due to:
    1. Repulsion: Ions are not perfectly rigid; they "squash" slightly at close range.
    2. Vibrational Energy: Kinetic energy of the crystal vibrations must be accounted for.

4. Electrostatic Energy in Nuclei

While nuclear forces (strong interactions) hold nucleons together, electrostatic repulsion between protons significantly impacts nuclear stability and energy levels.

  • Charge Independence: Nuclear forces between p−pp-p, n−nn-n, and p−np-n are identical in their complexity.
  • Mirror Nuclei: Nuclei like B11B^{11} (5p, 6n) and C11C^{11} (6p, 5n) show nearly identical energy level patterns, suggesting that the primary difference in their ground-state energy is electrostatic.
  • Nuclear Radius Calculation: By attributing the energy difference between mirror nuclei to electrostatic repulsion, the radius of the nucleus can be estimated.

5. Energy Density in the Electric Field

Electrostatic energy can be viewed not just as an interaction between charges, but as being located within the field itself.

  • Local Conservation of Energy: This principle suggests energy in a volume changes only by the amount flowing in or out.
  • Energy Density Formula: The energy per unit volume (uu) at any point in space is proportional to the square of the electric field: u=ϵ0E22u = \frac{\epsilon_0 E^2}{2}.
  • Total Field Energy: U=ϵ02∫𝑬⋅𝑬dVU = \frac{\epsilon_0}{2} \int \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{E} \, dV.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Capacity (C): The ratio of the charge on a conductor to the potential difference; C=Q/VC = Q/V.
  • Dissociation Energy: The energy required to completely separate the ions or molecules of a substance (e.g., pulling a salt crystal apart into individual ions).
  • Energy Density (uu): The amount of energy stored in a field per unit volume, calculated as ϵ0E22\frac{\epsilon_0 E^2}{2}.
  • Ground State: The configuration of a system (like an atomic nucleus) that has the lowest possible energy.
  • Ionic Lattice: A regular, repeating three-dimensional arrangement of positive and negative ions in a crystal.
  • Local Conservation of Energy: The principle that energy in a specific volume changes only by the amount that flows across its boundaries.
  • Mirror Nuclei: Pairs of nuclei where the number of protons in one equals the number of neutrons in the other (e.g., B11B^{11} and C11C^{11}).
  • Principle of Superposition: The total force on any charge is the vector sum of the forces exerted by all other individual charges in the system.
  • Virtual Work: A method for finding forces by assuming a small "virtual" displacement and calculating the resulting change in the system's potential energy.

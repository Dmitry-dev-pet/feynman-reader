Chapter 20: Solutions of Maxwell’s Equations in Free Space

This briefing document provides an analytical overview of the behavior of electromagnetic fields in free space, as derived from Maxwell’s equations. It covers the mathematical derivation of wave equations, the physical characteristics of plane and spherical waves, and the philosophical demands placed on scientific imagination when conceptualizing these phenomena.

Executive Summary

The classical theory of electric and magnetic fields is encapsulated in four fundamental equations. When these equations are solved for free space (where charges ρ\rho and currents 𝒋\mathbf{j} are zero), they reveal a remarkable phenomenon: electromagnetic fields can exist independently of their sources, traveling through space as waves at the speed of light (cc). These waves can be analyzed as one-dimensional plane waves, three-dimensional superpositions, or spherical waves radiating from a central point. The study of these solutions demonstrates that light, radio waves, and other forms of radiation are all manifestations of the same underlying electromagnetic field, which satisfies the three-dimensional wave equation.

Key Themes and Analytical Deep Dive

1. The Emergence of Waves in Free Space

Maxwell’s equations imply that fields generated by moving charges can "leave" their sources. A primary example is an infinite current sheet in the yzyz-plane. When a current density JJ is turned on, it produces uniform electric (EE) and magnetic (BB) fields that propagate in the xx-direction.

  • Field Components: For a current in the yy-direction, EE has only a yy-component and BB has only a zz-component.
  • Fundamental Relationship: Ey=cBz=−J2ϵ0cE_y = cB_z = -\frac{J}{2\epsilon_0 c}.
  • Propagation: The "wavefront" moves outward at velocity cc. Even after the source current is turned off, a "block" of field continues to travel through space, preserving a "memory" of the source's history.

2. The One-Dimensional Wave Equation

In a vacuum, and assuming fields vary only in the xx-direction (plane waves), Maxwell’s equations simplify to a second-order partial differential equation.

The 1D Wave Equation: ∂2ψ∂x2−1c2∂2ψ∂t2=0\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x^2} - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial t^2} = 0

General Solutions: The most general solution is the superposition of two arbitrary functions: ψ=f(x−ct)+g(x+ct)\psi = f(x - ct) + g(x + ct)

  • f(x−ct)f(x - ct): A wave traveling in the positive xx-direction.
  • g(x+ct)g(x + ct): A wave traveling in the negative xx-direction.

Physical Properties of Plane Waves:

  • Transversality: The electric field 𝑬\mathbf{E} must be at right angles to the direction of propagation.
  • Orthogonality: 𝑬\mathbf{E} and 𝑩\mathbf{B} are perpendicular to each other.
  • Direction of Travel: The cross product 𝑬×𝑩\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B} points in the direction of wave propagation.

3. Three-Dimensional Generalization

The vector 𝑬\mathbf{E} and the vector 𝑩\mathbf{B} both satisfy the three-dimensional wave equation in free space: ∇2𝑬−1c2∂2𝑬∂t2=0\nabla^2\mathbf{E} - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} = 0

The general solution is a superposition of an infinite variety of plane waves moving in all possible directions. This accounts for the incredible complexity of fields in any given space (e.g., a lecture room), which contain simultaneous signals from local wiring, earth’s magnetism, light, radio broadcasts, and distant cosmic sources.

4. Spherical Waves and Energy Conservation

Spherical waves are solutions that spread out from a point source. To analyze these, the Laplacian is expressed in terms of the radial distance rr.

The Spherical Wave Equation: ∂2∂r2(rψ)−1c2∂2∂t2(rψ)=0\frac{\partial^2}{\partial r^2}(r\psi) - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}(r\psi) = 0

The Solution: ψ=f(t−r/c)r\psi = \frac{f(t - r/c)}{r}

Important Quotes with Context

On the Reality of Fields

"What we call the stars are only inferences, inferences drawn from the only physical reality we have yet gotten from them—from a careful study of the unendingly complex undulations of the electric and magnetic fields reaching us on earth."

Context: Feynman explains that while we perceive "objects" like stars, our physical connection to the universe is mediated entirely by the electromagnetic fields that satisfy the wave equation.

On scientific Imagination

"Our science makes terrific demands on the imagination... whatever we are allowed to imagine in science must be consistent with everything else we know... our kind of imagination is quite a difficult game. One has to have the imagination to think of something that has never been seen before... at the same time the thoughts are restricted in a strait jacket."

Context: This addresses the difficulty of visualizing fields. Unlike "invisible angels," fields must adhere strictly to mathematical laws, limiting the freedom of the imaginer to only that which is physically consistent.

On Mathematical Beauty

"In the wave equation... there’s something nice about the regularity of the appearance of the x, the y, the z, and the t. And this nice symmetry... suggests to the mind still a greater beauty which has to do with the four dimensions."

Context: Feynman notes that even if one cannot "see" a field, the mathematical symmetry of the equations provides an intellectual and aesthetic satisfaction linked to the theory of relativity.

Actionable Insights for Physical Interpretation

  1. Independent Existence: Once generated, electromagnetic fields require no medium (like "jello" or "ether") and no continuing source activity to propagate through space.
  2. Superposition Principle: Because Maxwell's equations are linear, complex field environments are simply the mathematical sum of various independent wave solutions.
  3. Physical Limits of Visualization: One should not be discouraged by the inability to "visualize" a field. The most accurate representation is often the mathematical one—six numbers (three for EE, three for BB) associated with every point in space.
  4. Time Asymmetry: Although the wave equations are mathematically symmetric regarding the sign of time (tt), "physical sense" dictates choosing the outgoing wave solution for sources, representing an empirical addition to the basic Maxwellian laws.
  5. Source Identification: A solution to the "free space" wave equation that becomes infinite at the origin (like the 1/r1/r spherical wave) implies the necessary existence of a source (charge or current) at that origin, even if the equation was originally solved for "zero" sources.

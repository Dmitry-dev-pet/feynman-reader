Study Guide: Electrodynamics in Relativistic Notation

This study guide covers the application of the special theory of relativity to electrodynamics, focusing on the use of four-vectors, the four-dimensional gradient, and the invariant formulation of Maxwell’s equations.

Key Concepts

1. Relativity and Units

The laws of physics must remain invariant under Lorentz transformations. To simplify the mathematical representation of space-time symmetry, a system of units is adopted where the speed of light c=1c = 1. In this system, time can be measured in meters (the time it takes light to travel one meter). To revert to standard units, tt is replaced by ctct.

2. The Four-Vector

A four-vector is a set of four quantities (at,ax,ay,az)(a_t, a_x, a_y, a_z), denoted as aμa_\mu, that transforms exactly like (t,x,y,z)(t, x, y, z) under a Lorentz transformation.

  • Four-Momentum (pμp_\mu): Composed of energy EE and the three-momentum vector 𝒑\mathbf{p}: pμ=(E,𝒑)p_\mu = (E, \mathbf{p}).
  • Four-Velocity (uμu_\mu): Since dtdt is not invariant, the standard velocity components must be divided by 1−v2\sqrt{1-v^2} to form a four-vector: uμ=(11−v2,𝒗1−v2)u_\mu = \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-v^2}}, \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\sqrt{1-v^2}} \right).
  • Four-Potential (AμA_\mu): The scalar potential ϕ\phi and vector potential 𝑨\mathbf{A} combine to form Aμ=(ϕ,𝑨)A_\mu = (\phi, \mathbf{A}).
  • Current Density Four-Vector (jμj_\mu): Composed of charge density ρ\rho and current density 𝒋\mathbf{j}: jμ=(ρ,𝒋)j_\mu = (\rho, \mathbf{j}).

3. The Scalar Product and Invariance

In four dimensions, the quantity t2−x2−y2−z2t^2 - x^2 - y^2 - z^2 is invariant under the complete Lorentz group (translations and rotations). For any two four-vectors aμa_\mu and bμb_\mu, the scalar product is defined as: aμbμ=atbt−axbx−ayby−azbz=atbt−𝒂⋅𝒃a_\mu b_\mu = a_t b_t - a_x b_x - a_y b_y - a_z b_z = a_t b_t - \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} The "length" of a four-vector squared is aμaμ=at2−𝒂2a_\mu a_\mu = a_t^2 - \mathbf{a}^2. For a particle with rest mass MM, the invariant length of its four-momentum is pμpμ=M2p_\mu p_\mu = M^2.

4. Four-Dimensional Operators

  • Four-Gradient ($\fournabla$): To ensure proper transformation, the four-gradient is defined with a sign change in the spatial components: $\fournabla = (\frac{\partial}{\partial t}, -\boldsymbol{\nabla})$.
  • Four-Divergence: The dot product of the four-gradient and a four-vector field bμb_\mu: $\fournabla b_\mu = \frac{\partial b_t}{\partial t} + \mathbf{div} \mathbf{b}$.
  • d’Alembertian (□2\Box^2): The four-dimensional analog of the Laplacian: $\Box^2 = \fournabla \fournabla = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2} - \nabla^2$.

5. Invariant Formulation of Electrodynamics

Maxwell’s equations can be reduced to a single, elegant four-vector equation using the d'Alembertian and the four-potential: □2Aμ=jμϵ0\Box^2 A_\mu = \frac{j_\mu}{\epsilon_0} This is subject to the Lorenz condition (the four-divergence of the four-potential is zero): $\fournabla A_\mu = 0$.

Common Misconceptions

  • The Time Component of Velocity: It is tempting to assume the time component of velocity is vt=dtdt=1v_t = \frac{dt}{dt} = 1. This is incorrect because dtdt is not a Lorentz invariant. The correct four-velocity uμu_\mu requires dividing by 1−v2\sqrt{1-v^2} to ensure it transforms as a four-vector.
  • The Sign of the Four-Gradient: One might guess the four-gradient is (∂∂t,𝜵)(\frac{\partial}{\partial t}, \boldsymbol{\nabla}). However, the spatial components must be negative (−∂∂x,−∂∂y,−∂∂z)(-\frac{\partial}{\partial x}, -\frac{\partial}{\partial y}, -\frac{\partial}{\partial z}) for the operator to transform correctly according to the Lorentz transformation.
  • Superficial Simplicity: The fact that equations can be written in a compact form (e.g., U=0U=0) does not necessarily mean they are simple. True physical simplicity arises when a notation (like four-vectors) reflects the inherent symmetries of nature (like Lorentz invariance).

Self-Check Questions

  1. Why is the four-velocity defined with a factor of 1/1−v21/\sqrt{1-v^2}?
    • Answer: Standard velocity components use dtdt in the denominator, which is not invariant under Lorentz transformations. Dividing by 1−v2\sqrt{1-v^2} (or multiplying by dt/dτdt/d\tau) ensures the components transform like (t,x,y,z)(t, x, y, z).
  2. How is the law of conservation of charge expressed in relativistic notation?
    • Answer: It is expressed as the four-divergence of the current density four-vector: $\fournabla j_\mu = 0$.
  3. What is the "length" of the four-velocity vector uμuμu_\mu u_\mu?
    • Answer: The length is always 1 (uμuμ=11−v2−v21−v2=1u_\mu u_\mu = \frac{1}{1-v^2} - \frac{v^2}{1-v^2} = 1).
  4. How do the scalar and vector potentials transform when moving to a frame S′S' with velocity vv in the xx-direction?
    • Answer: They transform like tt and xx: ϕ′=ϕ−vAx1−v2\phi' = \frac{\phi - vA_x}{\sqrt{1-v^2}} and Ax′=Ax−vϕ1−v2A_x' = \frac{A_x - v\phi}{\sqrt{1-v^2}}.
  5. What is the significance of the d’Alembertian operator □2\Box^2?
    • Answer: It is an invariant scalar operator that allows Maxwell’s equations for potentials to be written in a form that is clearly valid in all inertial frames.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Center-of-Mass (CM) System: A coordinate system where the total three-momentum of all particles in a reaction is zero.
  • d’Alembertian (□2\Box^2): A four-dimensional differential operator defined as the second time derivative minus the Laplacian; it is invariant under Lorentz transformations.
  • Four-Potential (AμA_\mu): A four-vector field whose components are the scalar potential ϕ\phi and the three-vector potential 𝑨\mathbf{A}.
  • Four-Vector: A set of four quantities that transform under a Lorentz transformation in the same way as the space-time coordinates (t,x,y,z)(t, x, y, z).
  • Invariant Scalar: A quantity (such as rest mass m0m_0 or the scalar product aμbμa_\mu b_\mu) that has the same numerical value in all inertial coordinate systems.
  • Lorentz Transformation: The set of equations relating the space and time coordinates of two inertial systems in relative motion.
  • Lorenz Condition: The requirement that the four-divergence of the four-potential be zero ($\fournabla A_\mu = 0$), which simplifies the wave equations for potentials.
  • Scalar Product (Four-Dimensional): The invariant combination of two four-vectors aμa_\mu and bμb_\mu given by atbt−𝒂⋅𝒃a_t b_t - \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}.

Analytical Briefing: The Relation of Physics to Other Sciences

Executive Summary

This document provides a comprehensive analysis of the interdisciplinary relationships between physics and other major scientific fields, based on the foundational principles outlined in Chapter 3 of the Feynman Lectures on Physics.

Physics is characterized as the most fundamental and all-inclusive science, serving as the modern successor to "natural philosophy." While other sciences such as chemistry, biology, astronomy, and geology focus on specific domains of the natural world, they all rely on the underlying laws of physics. The briefing highlights that while theoretical foundations (like quantum mechanics in chemistry) may be known "in principle," the complexity of nature often prevents direct calculation, necessitating specialized study in each field. Key focus areas include the physical machinery of living cells, the nuclear origin of elements in stars, and the persistent challenge of understanding turbulent flow in fluids.

Detailed Analysis of Key Themes

1. Chemistry: The Physics of Atoms

Chemistry is the science most deeply affected by physics. Historically, experiments in chemistry substantiated the atomic theory, which physics later formalized.

  • Inorganic Chemistry: The study of elements and simple compounds. All rules of chemical combination were ultimately explained by quantum mechanics. Thus, theoretical chemistry is, in principle, a branch of physics.
  • Statistical Mechanics: A shared discipline that applies mechanical laws to large numbers of atoms. It explains the phenomena of heat and thermodynamics through the "random and complicated" jiggling of molecules.
  • Organic Chemistry: The study of substances associated with living things. It was once believed these substances required a "vital force," but they are now understood as complex arrangements of the same atoms found in inorganic chemistry.

2. Biology: The Machinery of Life

Biology has transitioned from a descriptive science to a study of molecular machinery. The fundamental assumption driving modern biology is that "everything that living things do can be understood in terms of the jigglings and wigglings of atoms."

  • Conservation of Energy: First demonstrated in a biological context by Mayer, regarding the heat exchange of living creatures.
  • Nervous System as Electrical Circuit: Nerves function like capacitors. A wave of "penetrability" (the nerve impulse) travels down a fiber, driven by the pumping of ions across a thin membrane.
  • The Krebs Cycle (Figure 3-1): A respiratory cycle where molecules change in a sequence of small steps. This process facilitates energy storage and transfer, specifically the transformation of GDP (guanosine-di-phosphate) to GTP (guanosine-tri-phosphate). GTP acts as an energy-carrying "box" used to drive mechanical actions like muscle contraction.
  • Enzymes and Proteins: Enzymes are large protein molecules that act as catalysts. They lower the "activation energy" or "hill" of a reaction, allowing it to proceed without extra energy.
  • DNA: The Genetic Blueprint (Figure 3-2): DNA carries the instructions for making enzymes. Its structure consists of a double-chain backbone of sugar and phosphate groups, connected by complementary pairs of cross-links (Adenine-Thymine, Cytosine-Guanine).

3. Astronomy: The Nuclear Laboratory

Astronomy provided the initial spark for physics by demonstrating the simplicity of planetary motion.

  • Universal Composition: Spectroscopy has proven that stars are made of the same atoms found on Earth (e.g., Helium was discovered on the sun before it was found on Earth).
  • Nuclear Synthesis: Stars are powered by nuclear "burning," primarily the conversion of hydrogen into helium. Heavier elements are "cooked" in the centers of stars and distributed throughout the universe via novae and supernovae.
  • Isotopes as Clues: The proportions of isotopes (like C12C^{12} and C13C^{13}) serve as a "nuclear fingerprint," allowing scientists to understand the conditions of the stellar furnaces that created the matter of our world.

4. Geology and Meteorology: The Challenge of Complexity

These fields deal with the "Earth sciences," where physics is used for instrumentation but the theory remains incomplete due to the complexity of the systems involved.

  • Turbulence and Instability: Meteorologists cannot accurately predict weather because the motion of air is unstable and sensitive. This "turbulent flow" remains an unsolved problem in physics.
  • Internal Earth Dynamics: While the speed of earthquake waves is known, there is no complete theory for the density of matter at the extreme pressures found at the Earth's center, nor for the circulating currents that drive mountain-forming processes and volcanism.

5. Psychology: The Physical Basis of Mind

The briefing distinguishes between psychoanalysis (which it categorizes as non-scientific due to a lack of experimental verification) and the physiology of sensation.

  • The Memory Problem: The central unsolved problem in psychology/biology is the physical nature of memory. If the brain is made of atoms, learning must involve a physical change in the nervous system, yet it is currently unknown what that change is or where to look for it.

Important Quotes with Context

"Theoretical chemistry is in fact physics... but we must emphasize that this explanation is in principle."

  • Context: Discussing the reduction of Mendeleev’s periodic table and chemical rules to quantum mechanics. Knowing the rules of the "game" (physics) does not immediately make one a master "player" (chemist).

"Everything that living things do can be understood in terms of the jigglings and wigglings of atoms."

  • Context: Defining the "most powerful assumption" of modern biology, moving away from "vitalism" toward a physical-chemical understanding of life.

"The stuff of which we are made, was 'cooked' once, in a star, and spit out."

  • Context: Explaining the origin of chemical elements through nuclear reactions in stars and their subsequent distribution via supernova explosions.

"If our small minds, for some convenience, divide this glass of wine, this universe, into parts... remember that nature does not know it!"

  • Context: The concluding metaphor of the chapter, arguing that the division of science into "physics," "biology," etc., is an artificial human construct; nature is a single, unified whole.

Actionable Insights for the Physicist

  • Language Barrier: For physics to assist other sciences, the object of study must be described in "physicist’s language"—specifically, the location and identity of the atoms involved.
  • The "Historical Question": Unlike other sciences (Biology/Evolution, Geology/Origin of the Earth, Astronomy/History of the Universe), physics does not currently study its own history. It assumes the laws of physics are constant. If these laws are found to change over time, physics will become a historical science.
  • The Problem of "Wet Water": A major unresolved task for physics is the mathematical analysis of circulating or turbulent fluids. This failure prevents the full understanding of stellar convection, weather patterns, and internal earth currents. Solving the pressure-flow relationship in a simple pipe remains a primary goal.

Briefing: Symmetry and Conservation Laws

Executive Summary

In quantum mechanics, conservation laws are deeply rooted in the symmetries of physical systems and the principle of superposition. While classical physics often treats conservation laws as starting points, quantum mechanics derives them from the requirement that certain operations—such as translations in space, rotations, or reflections—commute with the Hamiltonian of the system. This document analyzes the relationship between symmetry operations and conserved quantities (momentum, energy, angular momentum, and parity), utilizing specific case studies like the hydrogen molecular ion and the disintegration of the Λ0\Lambda^0 particle to illustrate these fundamental principles.

Fundamental Principles of Symmetry

The Symmetry Operator

A physical system is considered symmetric with respect to an operation Q̂\hat{Q} if that operation leaves the basic physical situation unchanged. Mathematically, this is expressed through the commutation of the operator Q̂\hat{Q} with the time-development operator Û\hat{U}.

If the physics is symmetrical under Q̂\hat{Q}, then: Q̂Û=ÛQ̂\hat{Q}\hat{U} = \hat{U}\hat{Q}

For infinitesimal times, this leads to the condition that the symmetry operator must commute with the Hamiltonian (Ĥ\hat{H}): Q̂Ĥ=ĤQ̂\hat{Q}\hat{H} = \hat{H}\hat{Q}

Symmetry and Phase Factors

If a state |ψ0⟩|\psi_0\rangle is an eigenstate of a symmetry operator Q̂\hat{Q}, such that Q̂|ψ0⟩=eiδ|ψ0⟩\hat{Q}|\psi_0\rangle = e^{i\delta}|\psi_0\rangle, this property is conserved over time. If the Hamiltonian is symmetrical under Q̂\hat{Q}, the state will maintain the same symmetry character (the same phase factor eiδe^{i\delta}) for all times. This is the bedrock of all quantum mechanical conservation laws.

Parity and Inversion

The inversion operator P̂\hat{P} (or parity operator) moves every point at (x,y,z)(x, y, z) to (−x,−y,−z)(-x, -y, -z). Applying this operation twice returns the system to its original state, meaning P̂2=1\hat{P}^2 = 1. Consequently, the eigenvalues for parity are: * Even Parity: P̂|ψ0⟩=+|ψ0⟩\hat{P}|\psi_0\rangle = +|\psi_0\rangle * Odd Parity: P̂|ψ0⟩=−|ψ0⟩\hat{P}|\psi_0\rangle = -|\psi_0\rangle

While parity is conserved in electromagnetism, gravity, and strong interactions, it is not conserved in weak interactions (such as β\beta-decay).

Angular Momentum and Photons

For a state with definite angular momentum mℏm\hbar about the zz-axis, a rotation R̂z(ϕ)\hat{R}_z(\phi) results in: R̂z(ϕ)|ψ0⟩=eimϕ|ψ0⟩\hat{R}_z(\phi)|\psi_0\rangle = e^{im\phi}|\psi_0\rangle

Circularly Polarized Light: * Right Circularly Polarized (RHC) photons carry +1+1 unit of ℏ\hbar. * Left Circularly Polarized (LHC) photons carry −1-1 unit of ℏ\hbar. * Classical light also demonstrates this; a circularly polarized wave driving an electron in a circle performs work (dW/dtdW/dt) and exerts torque (dJz/dtdJ_z/dt) such that dJz/dW=1/ωdJ_z/dW = 1/\omega.

Spin-One Constraints: While a general spin-one particle has three states (m=+1,0,−1m = +1, 0, -1), zero-mass particles like photons have only two states (m=+jm = +j and −j-j along the direction of motion). They lack the m=0m=0 state because they cannot be at rest.

Case Study: Disintegration of the Λ0\Lambda^0 Particle

The decay Λ0→p+π−\Lambda^0 \to p + \pi^- provides a practical application of angular momentum conservation and a demonstration of parity violation.

Angular Distribution Analysis

Assume a Λ0\Lambda^0 is completely polarized (spin “up”). When it decays, the conservation of angular momentum requires specific spin states for the resulting proton: 1. If the proton is emitted along the +z+z-axis, it must have spin “up” to match the initial Λ0\Lambda^0 spin (since the pion has spin zero and cannot contribute orbital angular momentum along the axis of motion). 2. The probability of the proton being emitted at an angle θ\theta is determined by two amplitudes: * aa: Amplitude for spin-up Λ0\Lambda^0 to decay into a spin-up proton. * bb: Amplitude for spin-down Λ0\Lambda^0 to decay into a spin-down proton.

The resulting probability distribution f(θ)f(\theta) is: f(θ)=|a|2cos⁡2θ2+|b|2sin⁡2θ2f(\theta) = \left|a\right|^2\cos^2\frac{\theta}{2} + \left|b\right|^2\sin^2\frac{\theta}{2} Or, simplified: f(θ)=(|a|2+|b|22)+(|a|2−|b|22)cos⁡θf(\theta) = \left(\frac{\left|a\right|^2+\left|b\right|^2}{2}\right) + \left(\frac{\left|a\right|^2-\left|b\right|^2}{2}\right)\cos\theta

Experimental Findings

  • Parity Violation: If parity were conserved, the amplitudes aa and bb would be equal (or a=−ba = -b), making the distribution uniform. Experiments show an asymmetry (α≈−0.62\alpha \approx -0.62), proving parity is not conserved in this weak interaction.
  • Spin Determination: The fact that the distribution follows a linear cos⁡θ\cos\theta dependency confirms that the Λ0\Lambda^0 has spin 1/21/2.

Key Quotes and Context

  • On the beauty of Quantum Mechanics: “The most beautiful thing of quantum mechanics is that the conservation theorems can, in a sense, be derived from something else, whereas in classical mechanics they are practically the starting points of the laws.”
  • On Symmetry and Passivity: “Making a reflection and waiting a while… is the same as waiting a while and then making a reflection. These should be the same so long as UU doesn’t change under the reflection.”
  • On the Parity Violation: “Although until a few years ago it was thought that nature always conserved parity, it is now known that this is not true… because the β\beta-decay reaction does not have the inversion symmetry.”
  • On Zero-Mass Particles: “Light is screwy; it has only two states. It does not have the zero case. This strange lack is related to the fact that light cannot stand still.”

Actionable Insights for Physical Analysis

  1. Identify Symmetry to Predict Conservation: If a Hamiltonian is unchanged by a displacement (space or time) or rotation, the corresponding momentum, energy, or angular momentum is a “constant of the motion.”
  2. Use Parity to Simplify States: In systems dominated by electromagnetic or strong forces, states of definite energy that are non-degenerate must have a definite parity (even or odd). This can be used to rule out certain transitions or outcomes.
  3. Angular Momentum Conservation in Decays: When analyzing particle disintegrations, the total JzJ_z must remain constant. Use this to determine the required spin orientations of daughter particles, especially when particles are emitted along the axis of quantization.
  4. Detecting Parity Violation: An asymmetric angular distribution in a decay (like the cos⁡θ\cos\theta term in Λ0\Lambda^0 decay) is a direct experimental signature of parity violation, typically indicating a weak interaction process.

Chapter 33: Polarization Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the principles of polarization as described in the provided text. It covers the nature of the electric vector of light, the mechanisms of polarization, and the quantitative analysis of light reflection.

I. Key Concepts

1. The Electric Vector and Superposition

Light is described by an electric field vector that oscillates in a plane perpendicular to the direction of propagation. The state of polarization is determined by the relationship between the horizontal (xx) and vertical (yy) components of this oscillation.

  • Linear (Plane) Polarization: Occurs when the xx and yy components are in phase (or have a phase difference that is an integral multiple of π\pi). The electric field oscillates along a straight line.
  • Elliptical Polarization: The most general case, occurring when xx and yy components are out of phase. The tip of the electric field vector traces an ellipse in the xyxy-plane.
  • Circular Polarization: A special case of elliptical polarization occurring when xx and yy components have equal amplitudes but a phase difference of 90∘90^\circ (or any odd integral multiple of π/2\pi/2).
    • Right-hand circular polarization: The vector rotates counterclockwise when looking at the light coming toward the observer.
    • Left-hand circular polarization: The vector rotates clockwise.
  • Unpolarized Light: Light in which the polarization state changes more rapidly (e.g., every 10−810^{-8} seconds) than can be detected. It results from the random superposition of light from many atoms, causing polarization effects to average out.

2. Mechanisms of Polarization

The text identifies several ways light becomes polarized or interacts with matter based on its polarization:

  • Scattering: When light hits air molecules, it causes charges to vibrate. At a 90∘90^\circ angle to the incident beam, the scattered light is polarized along the direction of vibration.
  • Birefringence (Double Refraction): Certain anisotropic materials have different indices of refraction depending on the direction of polarization.
    • Optic Axis: The direction in a crystal along which the response of electrons is unique (usually parallel to the long axes of asymmetric molecules).
    • Quarter-wave plate: A birefringent material of specific thickness that introduces a 90∘90^\circ phase shift between two perpendicular polarizations, turning linear light into circular light.
    • Half-wave plate: A thickness that turns the axis of linearly polarized light by 90∘90^\circ (when the incident light is at 45∘45^\circ to the optic axis).
  • Induced Birefringence:
    • Stress: Applying tension to certain plastics aligns their molecules, making them birefringent.
    • Kerr Effect: Applying an electric field to certain liquids containing dipole molecules aligns them, creating birefringence. A device utilizing this is a Kerr cell.
  • Absorption: Materials like tourmaline or Polaroid have different coefficients of absorption for different polarizations. Polaroid transmits light parallel to its "pass axis" and absorbs light perpendicular to it.
    • Intensity Rule: The intensity of light transmitted through a polarizer is proportional to cos⁡2θ\cos^2\theta, where θ\theta is the angle between the incident polarization and the pass axis.

3. Reflection and Brewster’s Angle

When light reflects off a surface, such as glass, it can become polarized.

  • Brewster’s Angle: The specific angle of incidence at which the reflected beam is completely polarized. This occurs when the reflected beam and the refracted beam form a 90∘90^\circ angle.
  • Physical Basis: Reflected light is generated by the oscillation of charges in the material. At Brewster’s angle, the direction of reflection coincides with the direction in which charges oscillating in the plane of incidence cannot radiate. Consequently, only light polarized normal to the plane of incidence is reflected.

4. Optical Activity

Optical activity is the rotation of the direction of linear polarization as light passes through a substance.

  • Molecular Asymmetry: This occurs in substances with molecules that lack reflection symmetry (e.g., corkscrew-shaped molecules like those in corn syrup).
  • Mechanism: An incident field in one direction (yy) drives electrons along a spiral, forcing them to move in a perpendicular direction (xx). Because of the time delay for radiation to travel across the molecular diameter, the xx-components do not cancel perfectly, resulting in a tilted resultant field.

6. Anomalous Refraction and Angular Momentum

  • Anomalous Refraction: Observed in crystals like Iceland spar. An unpolarized beam splits into an ordinary ray (follows Snell’s law) and an extraordinary ray (displaced and does not follow Snell’s law). These two rays are linearly polarized at right angles to each other.
  • Angular Momentum: Circularly polarized light carries angular momentum.
    • Right-hand circular: Energy ℰ\mathcal{E} carries angular momentum +ℰ/ω+\mathcal{E}/\omega.
    • Left-hand circular: Energy ℰ\mathcal{E} carries angular momentum −ℰ/ω-\mathcal{E}/\omega.
    • This is demonstrated by the torque exerted on an electron driven in a circle by the rotating electric field.

II. Common Misconceptions

  • Complex vs. Physical Fields: Complex numbers are used as a mathematical convenience to track phase differences. The real and imaginary components do not represent physical xx and yy coordinates; the actual measurable electric fields are the xx and yy coordinates plotted in the diagrams.
  • Creation of Light by Polarizers: It is often thought that polarizers only remove light. However, placing a third polarizer at 45∘45^\circ between two crossed polarizers increases the light transmitted. This is not because the third polarizer "creates" light, but because it resolves the field into a new component that can pass through the final sheet.
  • Convention Discrepancies: The text notes that its convention for right-hand circular polarization (counterclockwise looking at the source) is consistent with modern particle physics, but some older optics books use the exact opposite convention.

IV. Glossary of Terms

Term Definition
Birefringence The property of a material having two different indices of refraction depending on the polarization of light.
Brewster’s Angle The angle of incidence where the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular, resulting in a completely polarized reflected beam.
Extraordinary Ray A ray in a birefringent crystal that does not follow Snell's law and is displaced from the ordinary path.
Herapathite A salt of iodine and quinine used in small crystals to create the original Polaroid sheets.
Isotropic A material having the same physical properties in all directions; the opposite of birefringent materials.
Kerr Effect The phenomenon where an electric field induces birefringence in a liquid.
Optic Axis The direction in a birefringent substance along which the index of refraction is unique; often the long axis of asymmetric molecules.
Ordinary Ray The ray in a birefringent crystal that follows the standard laws of refraction.
Quarter-Wave Plate A sheet of birefringent material of a thickness that introduces a 90∘90^\circ phase shift between perpendicular polarization components.
Reflection Coefficient The ratio of the square of the reflected amplitude to the square of the incident amplitude, representing the fraction of energy reflected.

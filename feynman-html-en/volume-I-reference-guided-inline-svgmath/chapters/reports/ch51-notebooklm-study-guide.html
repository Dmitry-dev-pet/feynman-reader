Chapter 51: Waves — Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the complex phenomena associated with waves as outlined in the provided text. It covers bow waves, shock waves, waves in solids, and the intricate nature of surface waves in liquids.

I. Key Concepts and Principles

1. Bow Waves and the Shock Front

When a source moves faster than the wave velocity (cwc_w) of the medium, it produces a conical wavefront known as a bow wave or shock wave.

  • The Cone Angle: The half-angle of the cone (θ\theta) is determined by the ratio of the wave speed to the source speed: sin⁡θ=cwv\sin\theta = \frac{c_w}{v}
  • Cherenkov Radiation: This occurs when a charged particle moves through a medium (like glass) faster than the phase velocity of light in that medium. This generates a conical wave of light, which is used in high-energy research to determine particle speed.

2. Shock Waves and Wavefront Sharpening

In many media, wave speed depends on amplitude. In gas, higher pressure leads to adiabatic compression and increased temperature, which in turn increases the speed of sound.

  • Sharpening: Because the high-pressure region behind a wavefront travels faster than the front itself, it "overtakes" the front, causing the wavefront to become increasingly steep (a "sharp" front).
  • Sonic Booms: Any object moving faster than the speed of sound generates these waves automatically. Acoustically, the shock cannot be heard until it arrives because it travels faster than the signals ahead of it.

3. Bores in Channels

A "bore" is a water wave with a sharp front, often created by a piston or a sudden change in water height.

  • Physics of Bores: Long waves travel faster in deeper water. Consequently, irregularities in energy pile up at the front.
  • Conservation Laws: The speed of a bore (uu) can be derived using the conservation of matter and momentum.
  • Energy Loss: In strong bores or shock waves, energy is not conserved in the traditional sense; it is lost to "churning" (in water) or heat (in gases).

4. Waves in Solids and Seismology

Solids support two primary types of waves, whereas fluids generally only support one.

  • Longitudinal Waves: Compression waves similar to sound in air.
  • Transverse (Shear) Waves: Waves characterized by sideways distortion. These travel slower than longitudinal waves and cannot propagate through liquids.
  • Earth's Composition: Seismology uses these properties to map the Earth's interior. The absence of transverse waves in the Earth's core indicates that the center is liquid.
  • Normal Modes: The Earth has natural frequencies of oscillation (e.g., an ellipsoidal mode of ~54 minutes). Large events like the 1960 Chilean earthquake allow these modes to be measured.

5. Surface Waves (Water Waves)

Water waves are complex because they are neither purely longitudinal nor purely transverse; particles move in circular orbits.

  • Gravity Waves: In deep water, the restoring force is gravity. Phase velocity (vphasev_{phase}) is proportional to λ\sqrt{\lambda} (wavelength).
  • Capillary Waves (Ripples): For very short wavelengths, surface tension is the restoring force. Here, vphasev_{phase} is proportional to 1/λ1/\sqrt{\lambda}, meaning shorter waves travel faster.
  • Dispersion: Because velocity depends on wavelength, different components of a disturbance travel at different speeds, spreading the wave out over time.

III. Common Misconceptions

  • Misconception: Nothing can move faster than light.
    • Reality: While nothing moves faster than light in a vacuum, particles can move faster than the phase velocity of light in a medium (like glass or water), resulting in Cherenkov radiation.
  • Misconception: Water waves are simple examples of transverse waves.
    • Reality: Water waves are highly complex. Particles move in circles, and the wave speed depends on wavelength, depth, and surface tension.
  • Misconception: Sound waves always travel at a constant speed in a medium.
    • Reality: In high-pressure scenarios (like explosions), the sound speed increases with the pressure/temperature of the wave, leading to the formation of shock waves.
  • Misconception: Energy is always conserved in wave propagation.
    • Reality: In shock waves and bores, energy is converted into heat or turbulent "churning," representing a net loss of organized wave energy.

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Adiabatic Compression: Compression of a gas where heat is not exchanged with the surroundings, resulting in a temperature increase.
  • Bore: A sudden, sharp-fronted wave in a channel, often involving turbulent water motion and energy loss.
  • Capillary Attraction: The restoring force in short-wavelength water ripples caused by surface tension.
  • Coriolis Force: A force resulting from the Earth's rotation that may cause the "splitting" of the Earth’s natural oscillation frequencies (spectral doublets).
  • Dispersion: The phenomenon where the phase velocity of a wave depends on its wavelength or frequency.
  • Fourier Analysis: A mathematical method used to break down a complex, irregular signal (like a seismograph tracing) into its constituent sine wave frequencies.
  • Group Velocity: The speed at which the overall envelope of a wave packet (and its energy) travels.
  • Longitudinal Wave: A wave where the particle motion is in the same direction as the wave propagation (e.g., sound).
  • Phase Velocity: The speed at which an individual wave crest or a specific phase of the wave travels.
  • Shear Wave (Transverse Wave): A wave in a solid where the motion of the material is perpendicular to the direction of the wave's travel.
  • Shock Wave: A high-amplitude wave moving faster than the local speed of sound, characterized by a sudden change in pressure, density, and temperature.

Chapter 14: The Magnetic Field in Various Situations

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of magnetostatics, focusing on the vector potential, the mathematical analogies between electrostatics and magnetostatics, and the specific magnetic fields produced by various current configurations.

Key Concepts

1. The Vector Potential (𝑨\mathbf{A})

In magnetostatics, the divergence of the magnetic field (𝑩\mathbf{B}) is always zero (div𝑩=0\text{div} \mathbf{B} = 0). This allows 𝑩\mathbf{B} to be represented as the curl of another vector field, known as the vector potential: 𝑩=curl𝑨\mathbf{B} = \text{curl} \mathbf{A} While the scalar potential ϕ\phi in electrostatics is determined by a gradient, the vector potential is determined by its curl. This definition allows for "gauge latitude"—adding the gradient of any scalar field to 𝑨\mathbf{A} does not change the resulting 𝑩\mathbf{B} field. For mathematical convenience in magnetostatics, the divergence of 𝑨\mathbf{A} is typically set to zero (div𝑨=0\text{div} \mathbf{A} = 0).

3. Magnetic Dipoles

A small loop of current II with area a×ba \times b behaves at large distances as a magnetic dipole.

  • Magnetic Dipole Moment (μ\mu): Defined as the current times the area of the loop (μ=I⋅Area\mu = I \cdot \text{Area}).
  • Vector Potential of a Dipole: 𝑨=14πϵ0c2𝝁×𝒆RR2\mathbf{A} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 c^2} \frac{\boldsymbol{\mu} \times \mathbf{e}_R}{R^2}.
  • Field Characteristics: Far from the source, the 𝑩\mathbf{B} field components of a magnetic dipole are mathematically identical to the 𝑬\mathbf{E} field components of an electric dipole.

4. The Law of Biot and Savart

While 𝑩\mathbf{B} can be found by taking the curl of 𝑨\mathbf{A}, it can also be calculated directly from the current distribution using the Biot-Savart Law: 𝑩(1)=14πϵ0c2∫𝒋(2)×𝒆12r122dV2\mathbf{B}(1) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 c^2} \int \frac{\mathbf{j}(2) \times \mathbf{e}_{12}}{r_{12}^2} dV_2 For thin wires, the term 𝒋dV\mathbf{j} dV is replaced by Id𝒔I d\mathbf{s}. Although this direct integral exists, it is often more complex to evaluate than the vector potential due to the cross-product within the integral.

Common Misconceptions

  • Physical Reality of 𝑨\mathbf{A} vs. 𝑩\mathbf{B}: It is a common misconception that if the magnetic field 𝑩\mathbf{B} is zero in a region, the vector potential 𝑨\mathbf{A} must also be zero. The example of a long solenoid proves this false: outside the solenoid, 𝑩\mathbf{B} is zero, but 𝑨\mathbf{A} is non-zero and circulates around the solenoid.
  • Relativity of Rotation: One might assume that a rotating charged cylinder can be analyzed from the frame of the cylinder to simplify the physics. However, a rotating system is not an inertial frame. The laws of electromagnetism as presented must be applied with respect to inertial coordinate systems.
  • Magnetic Poles: Unlike electric fields, which originate from point charges (monopoles), magnetic "dipole" fields are produced by circulating current loops. There are no actual magnetic "poles" equivalent to electric charges.

Self-Check Questions

  1. What is the circulation of the vector potential 𝑨\mathbf{A} around a closed loop equal to?
    • Answer: It is equal to the magnetic flux of 𝑩\mathbf{B} through that loop (∮𝑨⋅d𝒔=∫𝑩⋅𝒏da\oint \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{s} = \int \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, da).
  2. Why is div𝑨=0\text{div} \mathbf{A} = 0 chosen as a condition in magnetostatics?
    • Answer: It is a choice of mathematical convenience that simplifies the relation between the vector potential and current density to a Poisson equation (∇2𝑨=−𝒋/ϵ0c2\nabla^2 \mathbf{A} = -\mathbf{j}/\epsilon_0 c^2).
  3. For a uniform magnetic field 𝑩0\mathbf{B}_0 in the zz-direction, what is the simplest vector form of 𝑨\mathbf{A}?
    • Answer: 𝑨=12𝑩0×𝒓′\mathbf{A} = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{B}_0 \times \mathbf{r}', where 𝒓′\mathbf{r}' is the displacement from the zz-axis.
  4. How does the vector potential of a long straight wire behave outside the wire?
    • Answer: The zz-component AzA_z is proportional to the natural log of the distance from the wire (Az∝ln⁡r′A_z \propto \ln r').
  5. What happens to the magnetic field inside a long solenoid if it is replaced by a rotating charged cylinder?
    • Answer: It produces a similar magnetic field B=σaω/ϵ0c2B = \sigma a \omega / \epsilon_0 c^2, where σ\sigma is surface charge, aa is radius, and ω\omega is angular velocity.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Magnetostatics The study of magnetic fields associated with steady (constant) currents.
Vector Potential (𝑨\mathbf{A}) A vector field whose curl is the magnetic field (𝑩=curl𝑨\mathbf{B} = \text{curl} \mathbf{A}) and whose divergence is typically set to zero in magnetostatics.
Magnetic Dipole Moment (μ\mu) A vector representing the strength and orientation of a current loop; for a plane loop, it equals the current times the area.
Biot-Savart Law An equation that describes the magnetic field generated by a constant electric current in terms of an integral over the current distribution.
Surface Current Density (𝑱\mathbf{J}) The current per unit length flowing along a surface, such as the windings of a solenoid.
Gauge Latitude The freedom to add the gradient of a scalar field to the vector potential without changing the resulting physical magnetic field.
Solenoid A coil of wire, usually acting as an electromagnet, which produces a uniform magnetic field inside when carrying a current.

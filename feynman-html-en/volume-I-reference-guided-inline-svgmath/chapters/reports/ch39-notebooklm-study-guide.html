Chapter 39: The Kinetic Theory of Gases Study Guide

This study guide explores the properties of matter from an atomic perspective, specifically focusing on how the laws of mechanics (Newton’s laws) applied to atoms can explain macroscopic phenomena such as pressure, temperature, and the gas laws.

I. Key Concepts

1. The Nature of Pressure

Pressure is not a steady, continuous force but the result of a "perpetual bombardment" of atoms.

  • Microscopic Origin: Gas molecules in perpetual motion bang against surfaces (like a piston or an eardrum). Each collision delivers a small amount of momentum.
  • Definition: Pressure (PP) is the force (FF) applied to a surface divided by the area (AA) of that surface: P=F/AP = F/A.
  • Quantitative Analysis: For a gas in a box with a piston, the force on the piston is equal to the momentum delivered per second by colliding molecules. The resulting formula for pressure is: P=23n⟨mv22⟩P = \frac{2}{3} n \langle \frac{mv^2}{2} \rangle Where nn is the number of atoms per unit volume and ⟨mv22⟩\langle \frac{mv^2}{2} \rangle is the average kinetic energy of the center-of-mass motion.

2. Adiabatic Compression

An adiabatic process is one in which no heat energy is added to or removed from the system (no "heat leaks" through the walls).

  • Work and Energy: When a gas is compressed adiabatically, the work done on the gas (−PdV-P dV) goes entirely into increasing the internal energy (UU) of the atoms.
  • The Adiabatic Law: For a monatomic gas (where PV=23UPV = \frac{2}{3}U), the relationship between pressure and volume during adiabatic compression is: PVγ=C (a constant)PV^\gamma = C \text{ (a constant)} For monatomic gases, γ=5/3\gamma = 5/3.

3. Compressibility of Radiation (Photon Gas)

The kinetic theory can be applied to photons (particles of light) in a high-temperature environment, such as a star.

  • Momentum of Photons: Unlike atoms, photons travel at the speed of light (cc). Their momentum (pp) is related to their energy (EE) by E=pcE = pc.
  • Radiation Pressure: The pressure exerted by a photon gas is: PV=U/3PV = U/3 This leads to an adiabatic exponent of γ=4/3\gamma = 4/3 for radiation (PV4/3=CPV^{4/3} = C).

4. Temperature and Kinetic Energy

Temperature is a macroscopic measure of the microscopic motion of atoms.

  • Thermal Equilibrium: Two systems are at the same temperature when they have reached a state of equilibrium after interacting long enough.
  • Equipartition of Energy: In equilibrium, the average kinetic energy of atoms is the same regardless of their mass. Heavy atoms move slower, and light atoms move faster, but ⟨12m1v12⟩=⟨12m2v22⟩\langle \frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2 \rangle = \langle \frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^2 \rangle.
  • Absolute Temperature (TT): Defined such that the mean molecular kinetic energy is linearly proportional to the temperature: Mean Kinetic Energy=32kT\text{Mean Kinetic Energy} = \frac{3}{2}kT Where kk is the Boltzmann constant (1.38×10−23 J/K1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K}).

5. The Ideal Gas Law

Combining the definitions of pressure and temperature yields the ideal gas law:

  • By Particle Count: PV=NkTPV = NkT (where NN is the total number of atoms).
  • By Moles: PV=nRTPV = nRT (where nn is the number of moles and RR is the universal gas constant, R=N0kR = N_0k).
  • Avogadro’s Law: Equal volumes of different gases at the same pressure and temperature contain the same number of molecules.

6. Degrees of Freedom and Internal Motion

The total energy of a molecule is distributed among different types of motion.

  • Independent Directions: For every independent direction of motion (degree of freedom), the average kinetic energy is 12kT\frac{1}{2}kT.
  • Complex Molecules: A molecule with rr atoms has 3r3r degrees of freedom. The total mean kinetic energy is 32rkT\frac{3}{2}rkT.
  • Center-of-Mass (CM) vs. Internal Motion: Of the total energy, 32kT\frac{3}{2}kT is always associated with the bodily motion of the molecule's CM. The remainder, 32(r−1)kT\frac{3}{2}(r-1)kT, is associated with internal rotations and vibrations.

II. Self-Check Practice Questions

1. Why do we not hear the "perpetual racket" caused by air molecules hitting our eardrums? The atoms are so small and the sensitivity of the human ear is not quite high enough to notice the irregular "tattoo" of individual impacts. Additionally, there is normally an equal bombardment on both sides of the eardrum, resulting in zero net force.

2. What is the mathematical definition of Pressure in terms of force and area? P=F/AP = F/A.

3. In the formula PV=(γ−1)UPV = (\gamma - 1)U, what is the value of γ\gamma for a monatomic gas? γ=5/3\gamma = 5/3.

4. How does the pressure of a photon gas differ from that of a monatomic gas in terms of internal energy? For a monatomic gas, P=23(U/V)P = \frac{2}{3}(U/V), whereas for a photon gas, P=13(U/V)P = \frac{1}{3}(U/V).

5. What happens to the mean kinetic energy of a gas if the absolute temperature is doubled? The mean kinetic energy is linearly proportional to the temperature; therefore, doubling the absolute temperature doubles the mean kinetic energy.

6. What is the value of the Boltzmann constant (kk)? 1.38×10−231.38 \times 10^{-23} joules per degree Kelvin.

7. Define a "mole" as used by chemists. A mole is a specific number of objects, approximately 6.02×10236.02 \times 10^{23} (Avogadro's number N0N_0).

8. If a molecule has 3 atoms, what is its total mean kinetic energy in terms of kTkT? Using the formula 32rkT\frac{3}{2}rkT with r=3r=3, the total mean kinetic energy is 92kT\frac{9}{2}kT.

IV. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Physical Reality
Pressure is a constant, smooth force. Pressure is actually a series of discrete, irregular "kicks" or bombardments from individual atoms.
Heavier molecules in a mixture have more energy. In thermal equilibrium, the average kinetic energy is the same for all particles regardless of mass (12m1v12=12m2v22\frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^2). Heavier molecules simply move slower on average.
Work done during compression only increases temperature if heat is added. In an adiabatic compression, work done on the gas increases the internal energy (and thus the temperature) even though no heat is added.
The Ideal Gas Law only applies to single atoms. The law PV=NkTPV = NkT holds for complex molecules as well, because the pressure depends only on the center-of-mass motion of the molecules.

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Adiabatic: A process in which no heat is added to or removed from a system; all work done goes into changing the internal energy.
  • Boltzmann Constant (kk): The conversion factor between the energy of a molecule and a degree of absolute temperature (1.38×10−23 J/K1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K}).
  • Center-of-Mass (CM) System: A frame of reference in which the total momentum of the particles is zero, used to simplify the analysis of collisions.
  • Degrees of Freedom: The number of independent directions or ways in which a system can move or store kinetic energy.
  • Internal Energy (UU): The total energy of all the molecules within a gas or object.
  • Mole: A standardized unit representing 6.02×10236.02 \times 10^{23} objects (Avogadro's number).
  • Monatomic Gas: A gas consisting of single atoms (e.g., helium, argon, or mercury vapor) with no internal vibrations or rotations.
  • Photon Gas: A collection of photons, treated as a gas, typically found in high-temperature environments like stars.
  • Pressure (PP): The force per unit area exerted by the bombardment of molecules against a surface.
  • Thermodynamics: The study of the interrelationships between the macroscopic properties of substances, such as heat, pressure, and volume.

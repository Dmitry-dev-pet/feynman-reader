Study Guide: The Theory of Gravitation

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the principles of gravitation as outlined in the analysis of planetary motion, Newtonian dynamics, and universal physical laws.

I. Key Concepts

1. The Discovery of Planetary Laws

The understanding of gravitation began with a shift from philosophical debate to precise observation.

  • Tycho Brahe: Established the importance of careful experiment and measurement by recording voluminous tables of planetary positions.
  • Johannes Kepler: Analyzed Brahe’s data to formulate three remarkably simple laws:
    • First Law: Each planet moves in an ellipse, with the sun at one focus.
    • Second Law (Law of Areas): The radius vector from the sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time. This implies planets move faster when closer to the sun.
    • Third Law: The square of the orbital period (TT) is proportional to the cube of the semimajor axis (aa) of the orbit (T∝a3/2T \propto a^{3/2}).

2. Newtonian Dynamics and the Law of Gravitation

Isaac Newton synthesized Kepler’s observations with Galileo’s principle of inertia (an undisturbed object moves at a uniform speed in a straight line).

  • Force and Motion: Newton realized that a force is required to change either the speed or the direction of an object. Because planets do not move in straight lines, a force must be acting upon them.
  • Direction of Force: Newton proved that Kepler’s Law of Areas is a direct consequence of the force being directed radially toward the sun, rather than tangentially behind the planet.
  • The Inverse Square Law: By analyzing Kepler’s Third Law, Newton deduced that the gravitational force between two objects is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
  • Universal Law of Gravitation: Every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force defined by the formula: F=Gmm′r2F = G \frac{mm'}{r^2} where mm and m′m' are the masses, rr is the distance, and GG is the gravitational constant.

3. The Falling Moon and Earth-Bound Gravity

Newton connected the force pulling objects to the Earth’s surface (falling 16 feet in the first second) to the force holding the moon in orbit.

  • Centripetal Acceleration: The moon "falls" away from the straight line it would otherwise follow. At its distance (60 times the Earth's radius), the moon falls roughly 1/20 of an inch in one second, which aligns perfectly with the inverse square law (1/6021/60^2 of 16 feet).
  • Orbital Velocity: An object becomes a satellite if it travels horizontally fast enough (approx. 5 miles per second near Earth) that the Earth curves away from it at the same rate the object falls.

4. Applications of Universal Gravitation

  • Tides: Caused by the differential pull of the moon. The water closer to the moon is pulled more than the Earth’s center, and the water farther away is pulled less. This imbalance, combined with the Earth and moon rotating around a common center, creates two tidal bulges.
  • Planetary Shapes: Gravitation causes large masses (Earth, Sun, Moon) to attract themselves into spheres. Rotation causes a slight elliptical bulge at the equator.
  • Astronomy: Gravitation explains the "wobbles" (perturbations) in planetary orbits. The discovery of Neptune resulted from calculating where an unseen planet must be to cause irregularities in Uranus's orbit.
  • Stellar Clusters and Galaxies: Gravitation is observed acting across vast distances, holding together double stars, globular clusters, and entire spiral galaxies.

5. Cavendish Experiment: "Weighing the Earth"

Because the force of gravity between small objects is extremely weak, it was first measured by Henry Cavendish using a torsion fiber and lead balls.

  • Determining G: This experiment allowed for the calculation of the constant GG (6.670×10−11 newton⋅m2/kg26.670 \times 10^{-11} \text{ newton} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{kg}^2).
  • Mass of the Earth: Once GG was known, the mass of the Earth could finally be determined, as the Earth's pull on objects at its surface was already known.

6. Modifications and Relativity

  • Einstein’s Law of Gravitation: Newton’s law implies instantaneous action, which contradicts the principle that signals cannot travel faster than light. Einstein modified the law to account for these delays.
  • Energy and Mass: In relativity, anything with energy (including light) has mass and is affected by gravity. This was proven by observing the deflection of light by the sun during an eclipse.

II. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Fact
The moon does not fall toward Earth. The moon is constantly falling away from a straight-line path. It stays in orbit because its horizontal velocity is high enough that it "falls around" the Earth's curve.
There is only one tide per day. There are two tidal bulges. One is on the side facing the moon (stronger pull), and the other is on the far side (weaker pull than the Earth's center), caused by the Earth's rotation around a common center of mass.
Gravitation is a form of electricity. While both follow the inverse square law, they are distinct forces. Electrical forces are vastly stronger (by a factor of 4.17×10424.17 \times 10^{42}) and can repel, whereas gravity only attracts.
The gravitational constant GG changes as the universe ages. While some theories suggest this, evidence from solar brightness and Earth's geological history suggests GG has remained relatively stable; a 10% change would have made the early Earth too hot for life.

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Angular Momentum: The property of a spinning system that prevents it from contracting into a simple ball; responsible for the flattened, disc-like shapes of galaxies.
  • Centrifugal Force: An apparent force that "balances" gravity in a rotating frame, such as the Earth and moon rotating around a common center.
  • Ellipse: A specific mathematical curve (a foreshortened circle) where the sum of the distances from any point on the curve to two fixed points (foci) is constant.
  • Focus (of an ellipse): One of two central points used to define an ellipse; the sun sits at one focus of planetary orbits.
  • Inverse Square Law: A mathematical relationship where a physical quantity (like force) decreases in proportion to the square of the distance from the source.
  • Mass: A measure of inertia; fundamentally proportional to weight but distinct in that it measures how hard it is to change an object's motion.
  • Perturbation: A tiny deviation or "wobble" in a planet's orbit caused by the gravitational pull of other planets.
  • Radius Vector: An imaginary line connecting a central body (the sun) to a moving satellite (a planet).
  • Torsion Fiber: A very fine fiber used in the Cavendish experiment to measure extremely weak forces by observing how much the fiber twists.

Study Guide: Induced Currents

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the principles of electromagnetic induction, the mechanics of motors and generators, and the practical applications of induced currents as outlined in Chapter 16 of the Feynman Lectures on Physics, Volume II.

I. Key Concepts

1. The Principle of Reciprocity: Motors and Generators

  • The Motor Effect: A current-carrying wire placed in a magnetic field experiences a mechanical force. In a motor, a rectangular coil is placed between magnetic poles. When current flows, opposing forces on either side of the coil produce a torque, causing it to rotate.
  • The Generator Effect: When a coil is mechanically rotated within a magnetic field, the motion of the wires through the field produces an electromotive force (emf).
  • Equivalence: A motor and a generator are functionally equivalent; turning the shaft of a motor mechanically makes it a generator, while providing current to a generator makes it a motor. This reciprocity is fundamentally related to the law of conservation of energy.

2. Faraday’s Discovery of Induction

Prior to 1840, it was known that currents produce magnetic fields. Faraday discovered that magnetic fields could produce electric effects only when there is change. Induced currents are generated in three ways:

  1. Moving a wire through a magnetic field.
  2. Moving a magnet near a stationary wire.
  3. Changing the current in a nearby stationary wire.

3. The Flux Rule and Lenz’s Rule

  • The Flux Rule: The electromotive force (emf) generated in a loop is equal to the rate of change of the magnetic flux (the normal component of the magnetic field B integrated over the area of the loop) passing through it.
  • Lenz’s Rule: This rule determines the direction of the induced emf. An induced emf always opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it. If a current were to flow from that emf, it would create a magnetic field that resists the initial change in B.

4. Self-Inductance and Electrical Inertia

  • Self-Inductance: A changing current in a single coil produces a varying magnetic field within itself, which in turn induces a "back" emf.
  • Electrical Inertia: Self-inductance acts like mechanical inertia. It tries to keep the current constant, opposing increases in current and attempting to maintain current when it decreases.

5. Eddy Currents

When a bulk conductor (like a plate or disc) moves through a magnetic field or is exposed to a changing magnetic field, circulating currents called eddy currents are induced.

  • Viscous Drag: These currents create forces that oppose the motion of the conductor, acting like a viscous fluid (e.g., a copper pendulum slowing down when entering a magnetic field).
  • Reduction of Effects: Cutting slots into a conducting plate reduces the flux linkage and the available paths for current, thereby drastically reducing the drag force.

II. Self-Check Questions

Short-Answer Quiz

  1. What is a galvanometer, and upon what principle does it operate? A galvanometer is a sensitive instrument for measuring small electrical currents. It operates on the motor principle, where a coil mounted on delicate bearings or a fiber turns in response to the torque generated by a current in a magnetic field.
  2. What was the "missing feature" that early experimenters failed to realize before Faraday’s discovery of induction? Early experimenters looked for effects from static magnetic fields or constant currents; Faraday realized that electric effects (induction) only occur when there is change in the field or motion.
  3. Define electromotive force (emf) as it relates to a circuit. Emf is the tangential force per unit charge in the wire integrated over the length of the complete circuit; it represents the net "push" on electrons.
  4. How does a transformer "transform" voltage without a direct electrical connection? A transformer uses two coils. An alternating current in the first coil creates a continually changing magnetic field. This varying flux passes through the second coil, inducing an alternating emf in it via the flux rule.
  5. What happens when you try to suddenly open a switch in a circuit containing a large electromagnet? The rapidly decreasing current generates an enormous "back" emf due to self-inductance. This can cause an electrical arc across the switch contacts and potentially damage the insulation or cause injury.
  6. Why does a superconducting material repel a magnet? A superconductor has no resistance. Any attempt to introduce magnetic flux into it generates eddy currents that create an opposing field so perfect that no flux enters the material, resulting in a strong repulsive force.
  7. How does a "shaded-pole" induction motor create torque? An aluminum plate covers half of the magnet pole. Eddy currents in the plate cause the magnetic field in the "shaded" region to lag behind the "unshaded" region, creating the effect of a moving magnetic field that drags the rotor.

III. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
Static magnets produce electricity. A magnetic field must be changing or there must be relative motion between the field and the conductor to induce an emf.
Motors and generators are two different machines. They are the same device operated in different directions (mechanical to electrical vs. electrical to mechanical).
Eddy currents only occur in magnetic materials like iron. Eddy currents occur in any conductor (like copper or aluminum) when exposed to changing magnetic flux.
A transformer can work with a steady DC battery. A transformer requires a changing flux; therefore, it only works with alternating current (AC) or varying DC.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Physics of the Telephone: Using the principles of induction and magnetic attraction, explain how the human voice is converted into electrical signals and then reconstructed as sound in a standard telephone receiver.
  2. Engineering vs. Pure Physics: Feynman describes the transition from Faraday’s "useless" discovery to the construction of Boulder Dam. Discuss the role of engineering in optimizing basic physical principles, specifically regarding the use of iron and the minimization of gaps in electrical machinery.
  3. The Concept of Electrical Inertia: Compare and contrast mechanical inertia (an object's resistance to change in velocity) with self-inductance in a circuit. How does Lenz's Rule serve as the governing principle for this behavior?
  4. The Role of Eddy Currents in Technology: Analyze the dual nature of eddy currents—how they can be used constructively (as in induction motors or braking systems) and how they are mitigated when they are a source of inefficiency (as in the use of laminated iron or slotted plates).

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Eddy Currents: Circulating electrical currents induced within a bulk conductor by a changing magnetic field.
  • Electromotive Force (emf): The integrated tangential force per unit charge around a complete circuit.
  • Flux Rule: The principle stating that the induced emf in a loop is equal to the time rate of change of the magnetic flux through the loop.
  • Galvanometer: A device used to detect or measure small electric currents by observing the torque on a coil in a magnetic field.
  • Lenz’s Rule: The law stating that the direction of an induced current is such that it creates a magnetic field opposing the change in the original magnetic flux.
  • Self-Inductance: The property of a circuit where a change in current induces an emf in the same circuit, opposing the change in current.
  • Superconductor: A material that, at sufficiently low temperatures, exhibits zero electrical resistance and perfect diamagnetism (exclusion of magnetic fields).
  • Transformer: A device that transfers electrical energy between two or more coils through electromagnetic induction, typically used to change voltage levels.
  • Three-Phase Power: A system of electrical power generation that uses three symmetrical loops rotated together to provide a sequence of currents, often used to create rotating magnetic fields in industrial motors.

Study Guide: The Hydrogen Atom and The Periodic Table

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the quantum mechanical treatment of the hydrogen atom and the resulting theoretical framework for the periodic table of elements, based on the principles of Schrödinger’s equation.

I. Key Concepts

1. Schrödinger’s Equation for Hydrogen

To solve for the hydrogen atom, the proton is approximated as a fixed mass at the center. The electron is described by its amplitude to be at a position in space, ψ(x,y,z,t)\psi(x, y, z, t). The non-relativistic Schrödinger equation is: iℏ∂ψ∂t=−ℏ22m∇2ψ−e2rψi\hbar\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\psi - \frac{e^2}{r}\psi Because the potential energy V=−e2/rV = -e^2/r depends only on the radius rr, the equation is most effectively solved using spherical polar coordinates (r,θ,ϕr, \theta, \phi).

2. Natural Atomic Units

Calculations are simplified by measuring distance and energy in “natural” units: * Bohr Radius (rBr_B): The natural unit of length, approximately 0.5290.529 angstroms (ℏ2/me2\hbar^2/me^2). * Rydberg (ERE_R): The natural unit of energy, approximately 13.613.6 electron volts (me4/2ℏ2me^4/2\hbar^2).

3. The Radial Wave Function and Energy Levels

For bound states (where the electron is trapped by the proton), the wave function must go to zero as rr approaches infinity. This requirement forces the energy to be quantized. The allowed energies for hydrogen are: En=−ER(1n2)E_n = -E_R\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right) The principal quantum number (nn) is a positive integer (1,2,3,…1, 2, 3, \dots). The lowest energy (ground state) occurs at n=1n=1.

4. Quantum Numbers

Three integers characterize the stationary states of the hydrogen atom: * Principal Quantum Number (nn): Determines the energy level. * Orbital Quantum Number (ll): Determines the total orbital angular momentum. ll can range from 00 to n−1n-1. * Magnetic Quantum Number (mm): Determines the zz-component of the angular momentum. mm ranges from −l-l to +l+l.

5. Angular Momentum and Shells

The orbital angular momentum states are historically labeled with letters: * ss-states (l=0l=0): Spherically symmetric; the amplitude is non-zero at the nucleus. * pp-states (l=1l=1): Three possible states (m=+1,0,−1m = +1, 0, -1); has a nodal plane. * d,f,g,h…d, f, g, h \dots: Higher angular momentum states (l=2,3,4,5…l = 2, 3, 4, 5 \dots).

6. The Exclusion Principle and the Periodic Table

In multi-electron atoms, the Pauli Exclusion Principle states that each electronic state can be occupied by only one electron. Since an electron can have two spin orientations (up or down), each orbital defined by (n,l,m)(n, l, m) can hold two electrons. * Chemical properties are determined by the configuration of the outermost electrons. * Ionization Energy (WIW_I): The energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron. High WIW_I usually indicates chemical inertness (e.g., Noble Gases).

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why is the Schrödinger equation for hydrogen solved in polar coordinates instead of rectangular coordinates? Because the potential energy V=−e2/rV = -e^2/r depends only on the distance rr from the nucleus (a central field), making the Laplacian much simpler to handle in (r,θ,ϕ)(r, \theta, \phi).
  2. What physical condition on the wave function leads to the quantization of energy levels? The condition that the wave function for a bound electron must go to zero at very large distances (large ρ\rho); this requires the power series in the solution to terminate as a finite polynomial.
  3. What is the “pseudo-potential” or “centrifugal force” term in the radial equation? It is the term l(l+1)ℏ22mr2\frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2mr^2}, which accounts for the energy associated with orbital angular momentum, effectively pushing the electron away from the nucleus.
  4. How many pp-states exist for a given energy level n≥2n \geq 2? There are three states, corresponding to m=+1,0,−1m = +1, 0, -1.
  5. Why does the 2s2s state have a lower energy than the 2p2p state in lithium, even though they are the same in hydrogen? The 2s2s electron has a higher amplitude to be near the nucleus (penetration), where it feels the full charge of the nucleus (+3+3) rather than the shielded charge (+1+1) felt by the 2p2p electron.
  6. Explain the “break” in ionization energy trends between Nitrogen (Z=7Z=7) and Oxygen (Z=8Z=8). In Nitrogen, the three 2p2p electrons occupy x,y,x, y, and zz states separately to minimize repulsion. In Oxygen, the fourth 2p2p electron must pair up in an already occupied state, where it experiences strong mutual repulsion, making it easier to remove.
  7. Why is the water molecule (H2OH_2O) not linear? Oxygen has two vacancies in its 2p2p orbitals (e.g., in the xx and yy directions). Hydrogen atoms share electrons in these directed orbitals, which are at 90∘90^\circ to each other. Electric repulsion between the hydrogens then pushes the angle out to 105∘105^\circ.

IV. Common Misconceptions

  • “The electron orbits the nucleus like a planet.”
    • Correction: In quantum mechanics, the electron is described by a probability amplitude (a “cloud” or “blob”). There are no precise planetary-like paths.
  • “All states with the same principal quantum number nn have the same energy.”
    • Correction: This is only true for the hydrogen atom (ignoring small effects). In all other atoms, the interaction with other electrons causes states with different orbital quantum numbers ll (like 2s2s vs. 2p2p) to have different energies.
  • “Noble gases are completely inert because their shells are filled.”
    • Correction: While filled shells lead to great stability, some noble gases like Krypton can form weak compounds under specific conditions. Furthermore, having a filled shell is not the only requirement for stability; Beryllium has a filled 2s2s shell but is chemically active because the energy of that shell is high.
  • “The 3d3d shell always fills before the 4s4s shell.”
    • Correction: Due to the centrifugal force pushing higher ll states to higher energies, the 4s4s shell is actually lower in energy and fills before the 3d3d shell begins filling at Scandium (Z=21Z=21).

V. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Bohr Radius (rBr_B) The characteristic spreading distance of the 1s1s state of hydrogen (≈0.529\approx 0.529 Å).
Central Field A potential V(r)V(r) that depends only on the distance from a central point, making the Hamiltonian symmetric under rotations.
Degenerate State A situation where multiple different quantum states (different ll or mm) have the exact same energy.
Exclusion Principle The rule that no two electrons in an atom can occupy the same quantum state.
Ionization Energy The energy required to remove an electron from an atom; a measure of how tightly an electron is bound.
Legendre Polynomials Mathematical functions, Pl(cos⁡θ)P_l(\cos\theta), used to describe the angular dependence of wave functions when m=0m=0.
Nodal Surface A region (plane, cone, or sphere) where the probability amplitude ψ\psi of finding an electron is zero.
Orbital Parity A property of the wave function related to inversion; defined as (1)l(1)^l. For odd ll, the sign changes (odd parity); for even ll, it does not (even parity).
Rydberg (ERE_R) The ionization energy of the ground state of hydrogen (≈13.6\approx 13.6 eV).
Spherical Harmonics The set of functions Yl,m(θ,ϕ)Y_{l,m}(\theta, \phi) that describe the angular distribution of waves in spherical geometries.
Sub-shell A set of states within a shell that have the same nn and ll values, capable of holding 2(2l+1)2(2l+1) electrons.
